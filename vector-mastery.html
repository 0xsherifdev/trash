01 — Boolean Operations

Shape Math

Four operations that combine shapes. Think of it as set theory applied to geometry — every icon and logo you admire was likely built with these.

Union / Unite / Add

Merges all selected shapes into one combined shape. The outer boundary of every shape becomes the new outline. This is your go-to for building complex icons from simple primitives.

Use case: Combine a rectangle and a circle to create a pill/capsule shape, or merge overlapping forms to build a custom icon silhouette.

Subtract / Minus Front / Difference

The top shape cuts away from the bottom shape like a cookie cutter. Whatever overlaps is removed, keeping only the remaining part of the back shape.

Use case: Cut a circle from a rectangle to create a crescent moon, or punch holes in shapes for icons like a search magnifying glass.

Illustrator
PathfinderMinus Front

Intersect / Intersection

Keeps only the area where all shapes overlap. Everything outside the shared zone disappears. Think Venn diagram — only the middle part survives.

Use case: Create leaf shapes by intersecting two circles, or build complex symmetric patterns from overlapping geometry.

Illustrator
PathfinderIntersect

Exclude / XOR

The opposite of intersect — keeps everything except where the shapes overlap. The shared zone becomes empty. Useful for creating interesting negative space effects.

Use case: Create ring shapes, frame effects, or logos that play with positive and negative space.

PRO TIP

Figma's booleans are non-destructive. You can still select and move individual layers inside a boolean group. In Illustrator, use ⌘+Z immediately or use the Shape Builder Tool (Shift+M) for more flexible, visual merging.

02 — Geometry Tricks

Beyond the Basics

Techniques the tutorials skip. These will speed up your workflow and unlock shapes you didn't think were possible with simple geometry.

01

Shape Builder (Shift+M) in Illustrator

Draw overlapping circles, rectangles, and polygons. Select all, then press Shift+M. Click to merge regions, hold Alt/Option and click to subtract. Far more intuitive than Pathfinder for complex shapes.

Illustrator
02

Vector Networks in Figma

Unlike Illustrator's single-direction paths, Figma's vector nodes can branch in multiple directions. Draw a node and pull paths in any direction — perfect for complex line illustrations without separate paths.

Figma
03

Shift-Constrain for Clean Angles

Hold Shift while drawing or rotating to snap to 45° increments. This gives you perfectly horizontal, vertical, and diagonal lines every time. Works in both tools.

Both
04

Flatten then Simplify Paths

After boolean operations, paths often have too many anchor points. In Illustrator: Object → Path → Simplify. In Figma: Flatten Selection (⌘+E) then manually remove extra nodes. Fewer points = cleaner shapes.

Both
05

Divide & Trim (Pathfinder Extras)

Illustrator's Pathfinder has more than just the big four. Divide breaks overlapping shapes into separate pieces. Trim removes the hidden parts. Great for sticker-style illustrations.

Illustrator
06

Outline Stroke → Boolean

Convert a stroke to a filled shape (Object → Expand/Outline Stroke in Ai, or Outline Stroke in Figma). Now you can use boolean operations on it — perfect for making custom letterforms and logo marks.

Both
07

Radial Repeat for Mandalas

Draw one shape, then use Figma's new Repeat Transforms or Illustrator's Rotate Copy (rotate, copy, ⌘+D to repeat). Combine with booleans for intricate symmetric patterns and logos.

Both
08

Offset Path for Depth

In Illustrator: Object → Path → Offset Path creates a scaled copy at a set distance. Use this to add outlines, shadows, or layered depth effects to logos and icons without manually resizing.

Illustrator
03 — Logo & Brand Design

Principles That Stick

Great logos aren't accidents. They follow principles rooted in geometry, psychology, and restraint.

Simplicity

The best logos work at 16px favicon size and on a billboard. Strip away everything that isn't essential. If a child can draw it from memory, you're on the right track.

Scalability

Design in vector, test at every size. Your logo must work as a monochrome stamp, an app icon, a letterhead, and a sign. Avoid thin lines and tiny details that disappear at small sizes.

Geometry Grids

Build on a grid system. Use circles, golden ratios, and consistent proportions. Brands like Apple, Twitter, and Pepsi all built their logos on geometric foundations.

Negative Space

The space around and within your logo is a design tool. The FedEx arrow, the NBC peacock — negative space creates memorable, clever marks that reward a closer look.

Two Colors Max

95% of brands use no more than two colors. A signature color can boost recognition by up to 80%. Start in black and white — if it doesn't work mono, color won't save it.

Timelessness

Don't chase trends. Gradients, 3D effects, and drop shadows date quickly. Choose geometry that truthfully represents the brand and will still feel relevant in 20 years.

Shape Psychology in Branding

Every geometric shape carries subconscious meaning. Choose intentionally.

Circle

Unity, community, wholeness, protection, femininity

Square

Stability, trust, order, professionalism, strength

Triangle ▲

04 — Typography

Type Demystified

Typography spacing separates amateur work from professional design. Here are the three pillars, explained visually.

Kerning

Adjusts space between individual letter pairs. Critical for headlines, logos, and large type. Watch pairs like AV, WA, To, and LT — they always need manual attention.

Tracking

Adjusts spacing uniformly across all letters in a text block. Tight tracking feels formal and dense. Wide tracking feels open and airy — great for all-caps labels and navigation.

Leading

The vertical space between lines. The sweet spot is around 120–150% of the font size. Too tight makes text claustrophobic; too loose breaks reading rhythm.

RULE

The 120% leading rule: Set your line height to 120% of the font size as a starting point. For body text at 16px, use ~19px leading. For headings, tighten it to 100–110% since large text needs less breathing room.
